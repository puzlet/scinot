Scientific Notation

Many things in science and engineering are beyond everyday experience. For example, the Hitchhikers Guide to the Galaxy has this to say about the size of the universe:

Space is big. You just won't believe how vastly, hugely, mind-bogglingly big it is. I mean, you may think it's a long way down the road to the chemist's, but that's just peanuts to space.

— Hitchhiker's Guide to the Galaxy

With respect to The Guide, another description of the visible universe is its approximate diameter (in meters):

900000000000000000000000000

Scientists and engineers rarely use such long numbers— they are hard to read and to write.

Instead, we use scientific notation

9×1026

This roughly translates to: "a nine and twenty six zeros", and is said "nine times ten to the power of twenty six".

Understanding, practicing and applying scientific notation is the subject of this lecture.

Lets turn back to our example.

We can count the number of digits with a slider.

Counting digits shows about how big a number is.

For instance, a number with three digits is between 100 and 999—that is, in the hundreds. A number with four digits is between 1000 and 9999—in the thousands.

The key to scientific notation is to express a number in hundreds (if its three digits long) or thousands (if its four digits long) or millions (if seven), or whatever.

To do this for the number above (or any number), we write it as a product of a leading number and a multiplying power of ten (1, 10, 100, 1000, ...).

As the muliplying power of ten (the bottom number) is increased, we shift the decimal point on the top number.

When the decimal point is one digit from the left, we nearly have scientific notation.

All that remains is to convert the lower number into an exponential (a power of ten). (More on this in lecture 2.)
